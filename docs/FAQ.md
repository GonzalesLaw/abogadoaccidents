## Car Accident Basics

**Q: What should I do immediately after a car accident in California?**
A: Call 911. Seek medical attention even if you feel fine — adrenaline masks pain. Document the scene with photos and video. Get the other driver's name, license, insurance company, policy number, and license plate. Get witness contact information. Do NOT admit fault or apologize. **Q: How much is my car accident case worth in California?**
A: Settlement values depend on: total medical bills (past and future), lost wages and earning capacity, pain and suffering, permanent disability, property damage, and the clarity of liability. Minor injury cases may settle for $10,000–$50,000. Serious injuries (TBI, spinal surgery, permanent impairment) regularly reach six to seven figures. **Q: How long does a car accident settlement take in California?**
A: Simple claims with clear liability and minor injuries can resolve in 60–90 days. Moderate injury cases typically settle in 3–12 months. Serious injury cases requiring maximum medical improvement before settling can take 1–2 years. If a lawsuit is filed, cases can take 2–3 years to trial.

**Q: What is the statute of limitations for car accidents in California?**
A: 2 years from the date of the accident for personal injury claims. 6 months for claims against government entities (cities, counties, Caltrans). For minors, the clock starts at age 18. Do not wait — evidence disappears and witnesses forget. Call immediately.

## Liability & Fault

**Q: Who is at fault in a rear-end accident in California?**
A: In most rear-end accidents, the driver in the back is presumed at fault for following too closely. Exceptions exist if the front driver brake-checked, changed lanes unsafely, or had non-working brake lights. California follows pure comparative negligence — even if you are partially at fault, you can still recover compensation reduced by your percentage of fault.

**Q: What if the other driver has no insurance in California?**
A: Your own Uninsured Motorist (UM) and Underinsured Motorist (UIM) coverage can compensate you. Gonzales Law Offices reviews every available insurance source — your policy, the other driver's policy, third-party liability, and umbrella coverage — to maximize your total recovery.

**Q: Can I sue if I was partially at fault for the accident?**
A: Yes. California's pure comparative negligence law allows you to recover compensation even if you were 99% at fault — though your recovery is reduced by your percentage of fault. If you are 30% at fault and your damages are $100,000, you can still recover $70,000. Insurance companies routinely inflate your fault percentage to reduce their payout. Gonzales Law Offices fights to keep it accurately assigned.

**Q: What if the other driver fled the scene (hit and run)?**
A: Your own uninsured motorist coverage typically applies in hit-and-run accidents. You must report the accident to police immediately and preserve all evidence including surveillance camera footage from nearby businesses. Gonzales Law Offices has recovered compensation for hit-and-run victims across the Inland Empire.

## Insurance Tactics

**Q: How do insurance companies try to reduce my payout?**
A: Common tactics include: requesting recorded statements to use against you, claiming your injury is "pre-existing," using their own Independent Medical Examiner (IME) who downplays injuries, offering fast low settlements before you know your full damages, disputing causation, monitoring social media for activity that contradicts your injury claims, and delaying responses to exhaust your patience. Attorney Mark Gonzales worked in insurance defense — he knows every tactic.

**Q: Should I accept the insurance company's first offer?**
A: No. Initial offers are almost always lower than the true value of your case. Once you sign a release and accept a settlement, you cannot reopen the claim even if your injuries worsen. Never accept any offer without having an attorney review it first.

**Q: What is insurance bad faith in California?**
A: Insurance bad faith occurs when your own insurer unreasonably denies, delays, or underpays a valid claim. Under California law, you can sue your insurer for bad faith and potentially recover punitive damages far beyond your policy limits. California has among the strongest insurance bad faith laws in the nation.

**Q: Do I have to give a recorded statement to the insurance company?**
A: You are generally required to cooperate with your own insurance company but NOT with the other driver's insurance company. Do not give a recorded statement to any insurer without first speaking to an attorney. Call Gonzales Law Offices before making any statements.

## Injuries & Medical Treatment

**Q: What injuries are most common in car accidents?**
A: Whiplash and cervical strain, herniated and bulging discs, traumatic brain injury (TBI), fractured bones, torn ligaments and tendons, internal organ damage, spinal cord injuries, burns, and psychological trauma (PTSD, anxiety, depression). Symptoms may not appear immediately — always seek medical attention.

**Q: What if I didn't feel hurt right after the accident?**
A: Always seek medical attention even if you feel fine at the scene. Adrenaline and shock mask pain for hours or days. Delayed-onset injuries — especially whiplash, TBI, and soft tissue damage — are extremely common. A gap in medical treatment immediately after the accident is the #1 tool insurance companies use to deny claims.

**Q: How much is a whiplash settlement in California?**
A: Whiplash settlements range from $10,000 to $100,000+ depending on injury severity, treatment duration, whether surgery was required, and impact on daily life. Chronic cervical disc injuries can result in six-figure settlements. Gonzales Law Offices works with orthopedic specialists to document the full extent of whiplash injuries.

**Q: How much is a herniated disc settlement from a car accident?**
A: Herniated disc settlements range from $50,000 to $400,000+. Cases requiring epidural injections or physical therapy settle lower than cases requiring spinal fusion surgery or disc replacement. Future medical costs must be fully quantified before any settlement is accepted.

**Q: How much is a traumatic brain injury (TBI) case worth?**
A: TBI cases involving lasting cognitive, behavioral, or physical impairment regularly reach seven figures in California. Even "mild" TBI (concussion) causing chronic headaches, memory problems, or inability to work can yield substantial recoveries. Gonzales Law Offices works with neurologists and life-care planners to calculate the full lifetime cost of a TBI.

**Q: Can I get compensation for pain and suffering?**
A: Yes. California law allows full recovery for non-economic damages including physical pain, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life, and impact on personal relationships. There is no cap on pain and suffering in California car accident cases. These damages often exceed economic damages in serious injury cases.

## Special Accident Types

**Q: Who is liable in a semi-truck accident?**
A: Liability may fall on the truck driver, the trucking company, the cargo loader, the truck manufacturer, or all of them simultaneously. Federal FMCSA regulations govern hours of service, drug testing, and maintenance. Violations establish negligence per se. Black box data, driver logs, and dash cam footage must be preserved immediately — call Gonzales Law Offices before that evidence disappears.

**Q: What is the Uber insurance coverage in an accident?**
A: Coverage depends on app status: (1) App OFF: driver's personal insurance only. (2) App ON, no ride accepted: Uber provides $50K/$100K liability, $25K property damage. (3) En route or passenger in car: Uber's $1 million policy applies. Gonzales Law Offices navigates all rideshare coverage tiers for maximum recovery.

**Q: Can I sue Lyft if their driver caused my accident?**
A: Yes. When a Lyft driver has a passenger, Lyft's $1 million liability policy applies. Gonzales Law Offices has handled multiple Lyft accident cases in the Inland Empire and knows how to penetrate rideshare corporate defenses.

**Q: Can I sue for wrongful death after a car accident?**
A: Yes. Surviving spouses, children, and financial dependents can file wrongful death claims to recover: funeral expenses, lost financial support, loss of companionship, and in some cases punitive damages. The estate may also file a survival action for the deceased person's pain and suffering before death. You have 2 years from the date of death to file.
